



# Statistics — Bias & Variance

---

## Core Idea

A model's error comes from three sources:

$$ \text{Expected Error} = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance} + \text{Irreducible Noise} $$

|Term|Meaning|
|---|---|
|Bias|Error from wrong assumptions — model too simple|
|Variance|Error from sensitivity to training data — model too complex|
|Irreducible noise|Inherent randomness in data — cannot be reduced|

---

## Definitions

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Bias}(\hat{f}) &= \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)] - f(x) \\[6pt] \text{Variance}(\hat{f}) &= \mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{f}(x) - \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)])^2\right] \end{aligned} $$

Where $f(x)$ is the true function and $\hat{f}(x)$ is the model's prediction.

---

## Decomposition

The full MSE decomposition at a point $x$:

$$ \mathbb{E}[(y - \hat{f}(x))^2] = \underbrace{\text{Bias}(\hat{f}(x))^2}_{\text{systematic error}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}(\hat{f}(x))}_{\text{sensitivity}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{noise}} $$

---

## Intuition

|Property|High Bias|High Variance|
|---|---|---|
|Also called|Underfitting|Overfitting|
|Train loss|High|Low|
|Val loss|High|High|
|Train/val gap|Small|Large|
|Model|Too simple|Too complex|
|Example|Linear model on non-linear data|Deep net on small dataset|

---

## Visual Intuition — Dartboard Analogy

Think of each training run as throwing darts at a bullseye (true function):

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{High bias, low variance} &\quad \text{darts clustered, but off-center} \\ \text{Low bias, high variance} &\quad \text{darts centered on average, but scattered} \\ \text{High bias, high variance} &\quad \text{scattered and off-center — worst case} \\ \text{Low bias, low variance} &\quad \text{clustered at center — ideal} \end{aligned} $$

---

## The Tradeoff

Increasing model complexity:

$$ \text{Bias} \downarrow \quad \text{Variance} \uparrow $$

Decreasing model complexity:

$$ \text{Bias} \uparrow \quad \text{Variance} \downarrow $$

Optimal model sits at the sweet spot — minimum total error.

---

## Diagnosing from Loss Curves

|Symptom|Diagnosis|Fix|
|---|---|---|
|Train loss high, val loss high|High bias|Bigger model, more epochs, less regularization|
|Train loss low, val loss high|High variance|More data, dropout, L2, early stopping|
|Train loss low, val loss low|Good fit|Done|
|Val loss decreases then increases|Overfitting onset|Early stopping here|

---

## Fixes

### For High Bias (Underfitting)

- Increase model capacity (more layers, more parameters)
- Train longer
- Reduce regularization
- Add more features
- Use a more expressive architecture

### For High Variance (Overfitting)

- Get more training data
- Add dropout
- Add L1 / L2 regularization
- Early stopping
- Data augmentation
- Reduce model capacity
- Ensemble methods (bagging)

---

## Regularization and the Tradeoff

L2 regularization adds a penalty that shrinks weights:

$$ L_{\text{reg}} = L + \lambda |\mathbf{w}|^2 $$

|$\lambda$|Effect|
|---|---|
|$\lambda = 0$|No regularization — high variance risk|
|$\lambda$ small|Slight bias increase, variance decrease|
|$\lambda$ large|High bias — model becomes too simple|

$\lambda$ is a knob that moves you along the bias-variance tradeoff.

---

## Double Descent

Modern deep learning breaks the classical U-shaped curve.

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Classical regime} &\quad \text{test error increases after interpolation threshold} \\ \text{Modern regime} &\quad \text{test error decreases again with massive overparameterization} \end{aligned} $$

Overparameterized networks that perfectly fit training data can still generalize well — implicit regularization from SGD finds flat minima.

|Regime|Parameters|Behavior|
|---|---|---|
|Underparameterized|Few|Classical bias-variance tradeoff|
|Interpolation threshold|Just enough to fit|Worst generalization|
|Overparameterized|Many more than needed|Error decreases again|

---

## Ensemble Methods Reduce Variance

Average predictions of $M$ independent models:

$$ \hat{f}_{\text{ensemble}}(x) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M \hat{f}_m(x) $$

$$ \text{Var}\left(\hat{f}_{\text{ensemble}}\right) = \frac{1}{M} \text{Var}(\hat{f}) $$

Variance shrinks by $M$ — bias stays the same. This is why bagging and random forests work.
