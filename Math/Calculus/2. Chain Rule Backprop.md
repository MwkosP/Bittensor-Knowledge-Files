


# Calculus — Chain Rule & Backpropagation

---

## Chain Rule (1D)

When a function is composed of other functions, the derivative is the **product of their derivatives**.

$$ \frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) $$

Or in Leibniz notation — more intuitive for ML:

$$ \frac{dL}{dx} = \frac{dL}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} $$

Think of it as: how much does L change w.r.t. x = how much L changes w.r.t. y × how much y changes w.r.t. x.

---

## Chain Rule Example

$$ L = (3x + 1)^2 $$

Let $y = 3x + 1$, so $L = y^2$:

$$ \begin{aligned} \frac{dL}{dy} &= 2y \ \frac{dy}{dx} &= 3 \ \frac{dL}{dx} &= \frac{dL}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 2y \cdot 3 = 6(3x+1) \end{aligned} $$

---

## Chain Rule (Multi-variable)

When `L` depends on `y`, which depends on multiple inputs:

$$ \frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = \sum_i \frac{\partial L}{\partial y_i} \cdot \frac{\partial y_i}{\partial x} $$

**This is exactly what happens at every node in a neural network.**

---

## Computational Graph

Every neural network is a computational graph — a chain of operations.

```
x → [Linear] → z → [Activation] → a → [Loss] → L
```

Forward pass: compute values left → right.  
Backward pass: compute gradients right → left using chain rule.

---

## Backpropagation

Backprop is just the **chain rule applied systematically** through the computational graph.

### One Layer Example

$$ \begin{aligned} z &= Wx + b \quad &\text{(linear)} \\ a &= \sigma(z) \quad &\text{(activation)} \\ L &= \text{loss}(a, y) \quad &\text{(loss)} \end{aligned} $$

**Backward pass** — apply chain rule at each step:

$$ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial L}{\partial a} &= \text{(from loss function)} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial L}{\partial z} &= \frac{\partial L}{\partial a} \cdot \frac{\partial a}{\partial z} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial a} \cdot \sigma'(z) \\[6pt] \frac{\partial L}{\partial W} &= \frac{\partial L}{\partial z} \cdot \frac{\partial z}{\partial W} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z} \cdot x^\top \\[6pt] \frac{\partial L}{\partial b} &= \frac{\partial L}{\partial z} \cdot 1 \\[6pt] \frac{\partial L}{\partial x} &= \frac{\partial L}{\partial z} \cdot W^\top \end{aligned} $$

---

## Two Layer Example

```
x → [W₁, b₁] → z₁ → [σ] → a₁ → [W₂, b₂] → z₂ → [σ] → a₂ → L
```

$$ \begin{aligned} \delta_2 &= \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_2} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial a_2} \cdot \sigma'(z_2) \\[6pt] \frac{\partial L}{\partial W_2} &= \delta_2 \cdot a_1^\top \\[6pt] \delta_1 &= \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_1} = (W_2^\top \delta_2) \cdot \sigma'(z_1) \\[6pt] \frac{\partial L}{\partial W_1} &= \delta_1 \cdot x^\top \end{aligned} $$

The **error signal $\delta$** flows backward through each layer — hence "backpropagation".

---

## Vanishing & Exploding Gradients

At each layer, gradients get multiplied by weights and activation derivatives.

$$ \frac{\partial L}{\partial W_1} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial a_n} \cdot \prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial a_i}{\partial z_i} \cdot \frac{\partial z_i}{\partial a_{i-1}} $$

|Problem|Cause|Effect|Fix|
|---|---|---|---|
|Vanishing|Product of small values $< 1$|Early layers don't learn|ReLU, residuals, BatchNorm, good init|
|Exploding|Product of large values $> 1$|NaN loss, unstable training|Gradient clipping|

**Why ReLU helps:** $\sigma'_\text{ReLU}(z) = 1$ for $z > 0$ — doesn't shrink the gradient.  
**Why sigmoid hurts:** $\sigma'_\text{sigmoid} \leq 0.25$ — multiplied across layers → vanishes fast.

---

## Residual Connections

ResNets solve vanishing gradients by adding a shortcut:

$$ a = F(x) + x $$

$$ \frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial a} \cdot \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial x} + 1\right) $$

The `+1` guarantees gradient flows back even if $\partial F / \partial x \approx 0$.

---

## In Practice (PyTorch)

```python
loss.backward()   # computes all gradients via backprop
# gradients stored in param.grad for each parameter
optimizer.step()  # uses those gradients to update weights
```

PyTorch builds the computational graph dynamically during the forward pass, then traverses it backward.
