


# Calculus — Optimization Basics

---

## Core Goal

Find weights $\mathbf{w}$ that minimize the loss:

$$ \mathbf{w}^* = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w}} L(\mathbf{w}) $$

---

## Critical Points

A critical point is where the gradient is zero — the loss is flat.

$$ \nabla L(\mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{0} $$

There are three types:

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Local minimum} &\quad \text{loss curves up in all directions} \ \text{Local maximum} &\quad \text{loss curves down in all directions} \ \text{Saddle point} &\quad \text{curves up in some, down in others} \end{aligned} $$

---

## 1D Picture

For $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, use first and second derivatives:

|Condition|Point type|
|---|---|
|$f'(x) = 0$ and $f''(x) > 0$|Local minimum|
|$f'(x) = 0$ and $f''(x) < 0$|Local maximum|
|$f'(x) = 0$ and $f''(x) = 0$|Inconclusive|

### Example

$$ f(x) = x^3 - 3x $$

$$ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= 3x^2 - 3 = 0 \implies x = \pm 1 \\ f''(x) &= 6x \\ f''(1) &= 6 > 0 \implies x=1 \text{ is a local minimum} \\ f''(-1) &= -6 < 0 \implies x=-1 \text{ is a local maximum} \end{aligned} $$

---

## Multi-dimensional: Hessian Test

For $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, check eigenvalues of the Hessian $H$ at the critical point:

|Hessian eigenvalues|Point type|
|---|---|
|All $\lambda > 0$|Local minimum|
|All $\lambda < 0$|Local maximum|
|Mixed signs|Saddle point|
|Some $\lambda = 0$|Degenerate — flat direction|

---

## Saddle Points

A saddle point looks like a minimum from one direction and a maximum from another.

$$ f(x, y) = x^2 - y^2 $$

$$ \begin{aligned} \nabla f &= \begin{bmatrix} 2x \\ -2y \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{0} \implies (x,y) = (0,0) \\[6pt] H &= \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \implies \lambda_1 = 2,\\ \lambda_2 = -2 \quad \text{(mixed → saddle)} \end{aligned} $$

**In ML:** saddle points are extremely common in high dimensions. Gradient descent naturally escapes them due to noise in mini-batches.

---

## Local vs Global Minimum

$$ \text{Global minimum: } L(\mathbf{w}^*) \leq L(\mathbf{w}) \quad \forall \mathbf{w} $$

$$ \text{Local minimum: } L(\mathbf{w}^*) \leq L(\mathbf{w}) \quad \text{in a neighborhood} $$

**In ML:** we almost never find the global minimum — we don't need to. Modern networks are over-parameterized enough that most local minima are good enough.

---

## Convex vs Non-Convex

| Property | Convex | Non-Convex |
|---|---|---|
| Shape | Bowl — one minimum | Multiple minima, saddles, plateaus |
| Guarantee | Gradient descent finds global min | No guarantee |
| Examples | Linear regression, logistic regression, SVM | Neural networks |

A function is convex if:

$$ f(\lambda \mathbf{x} + (1-\lambda)\mathbf{y}) \leq \lambda f(\mathbf{x}) + (1-\lambda)f(\mathbf{y}) \quad \forall \lambda \in [0,1] $$

---

## Loss Landscape in Deep Learning

The loss surface of a neural network is extremely high-dimensional and non-convex. Key features:

|Feature|Description|
|---|---|
|Many saddle points|Most critical points — not minima|
|Flat regions / plateaus|$\|\nabla L\| \approx 0$, but not a minimum — training stalls|
|Sharp minima|Narrow valley — generalizes poorly|
|Flat minima|Wide valley — generalizes better|
|Loss barriers|High-loss regions separating minima|

---

## Learning Rate & the Loss Landscape

$$ \mathbf{w} \leftarrow \mathbf{w} - \eta \nabla L $$

|Learning rate|Effect|
|---|---|
|Too large|Overshoots minimum, diverges|
|Too small|Slow convergence, stuck in plateaus|
|Just right|Converges to a good minimum|
|Adaptive (Adam)|Adjusts per parameter — handles non-uniform curvature|

---

## Escaping Bad Points

|Problem|Solution|
|---|---|
|Stuck in plateau|Momentum carries through flat regions|
|Sharp minimum|Large LR or SGD noise finds flatter minima|
|Saddle point|SGD noise + momentum pushes away|
|Local minimum|Rarely a real problem in deep nets|

---

## Conditions for a Good Optimizer

1. **Reaches low loss** — finds a minimum
2. **Generalizes** — finds a flat minimum, not a sharp one
3. **Stable** — doesn't oscillate or diverge
4. **Fast** — converges in reasonable steps
