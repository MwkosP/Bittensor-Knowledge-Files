**Information theory** is the mathematical study of the [quantification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantification_\(science\) "Quantification (science)"), [storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_storage "Data storage"), and [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunications "Telecommunications") of a particular type of mathematically defined [information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information "Information"). The field was established and formalized by [Claude Shannon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Shannon "Claude Shannon") in the 1940s,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory#cite_note-1) though early contributions were made in the 1920s through the works of [Harry Nyquist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Nyquist "Harry Nyquist") and [Ralph Hartley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Hartley "Ralph Hartley"). It is at the intersection of [electronic engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_engineering "Electronic engineering"), [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics"), [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science"), [neurobiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuroscience "Neuroscience"), [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), and [electrical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_engineering "Electrical engineering").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory#cite_note-:2-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory#cite_note-:3-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory#cite_note-:0-4)

As a simple example, if one flips a fair coin and does not know the outcome (heads or tails), then they lack a certain amount of information. If one looks at the coin, they will know the outcome and gain that same amount of information. For a fair coin, the probability of either heads or tails is 1/2 and that amount of information can be expressed as −log2⁡(1/2)![{\displaystyle -\log _{2}(1/2)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33e01293d03ce1ddcb4450bab08b3b31ef4a0cf1) = 1 bit of information.

A key measure in information theory is [information entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_entropy "Information entropy"). Entropy is equal to the lack of information about a [random variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_variable "Random variable") or the outcome of a [random process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_process "Random process"). In the above coin flip example, the entropy in the case where you don't know the outcome is 1 bit. In the case where you do know the outcome, the entropy is zero. As another example, knowing the outcome of a [fair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fair_coin "Fair coin") [coin flip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coin_flip "Coin flip") (which has two equally likely outcomes) provides less information (lower entropy, less uncertainty) than identifying the outcome from a roll of a [die](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dice "Dice") (which has six equally likely outcomes). A simple way to look at information entropy in bits is approximately the average minimum number of yes/no questions you need to ask in order to recover complete information. (Exact for 2n possibilities and converges to exact for infinite possibilities). For the coin flip example, you only need to ask one question, e.g. "Is it heads?". "Yes" means its heads, "No" means it's tails, and you have your one bit of complete information.
