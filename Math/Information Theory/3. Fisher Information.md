




# Information Theory — Fisher Information

---

## Core Idea

Fisher information measures how much information data carries about a parameter $\theta$.  
"How sensitive is the distribution to changes in $\theta$?"

High Fisher information → small changes in $\theta$ cause large changes in the distribution → $\theta$ is easy to estimate precisely.

---

## Definition

For a distribution $p(x; \theta)$, the Fisher information is:

$$ \mathcal{I}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log p(x; \theta)\right)^2\right] $$

The term $\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log p(x; \theta)$ is called the **score function**.

Equivalent form (under regularity conditions):

$$ \mathcal{I}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \theta^2} \log p(x; \theta)\right] $$

Fisher information = **expected curvature** of the log-likelihood.

---

## Score Function

$$ s(\theta) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log p(x; \theta) $$

|Property|Formula|
|---|---|
|Expected score is zero|$\mathbb{E}[s(\theta)] = 0$|
|Variance of score = Fisher info|$\text{Var}(s(\theta)) = \mathcal{I}(\theta)$|

---

## Fisher Information Matrix (FIM)

For vector parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$ \mathcal{I}(\boldsymbol{\theta})_{ij} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \log p}{\partial \theta_i} \frac{\partial \log p}{\partial \theta_j}\right] $$

$$ \mathcal{I}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}\left[\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \log p \cdot \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \log p^\top\right] $$

- Always **symmetric** and **positive semi-definite**
- Generalizes scalar Fisher info to multi-parameter models
- Size: $n \times n$ where $n$ = number of parameters

---

## Cramér-Rao Bound

Fisher information sets a **lower bound** on estimator variance — no unbiased estimator can do better:

$$ \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq \frac{1}{\mathcal{I}(\theta)} $$

For the FIM:

$$ \text{Cov}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) \geq \mathcal{I}(\boldsymbol{\theta})^{-1} $$

High Fisher information → tight lower bound → parameter can be estimated precisely.

---

## Example — Gaussian

For $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, Fisher info for $\mu$ (known $\sigma$):

$$ \begin{aligned} \log p(x; \mu) &= -\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} + \text{const} \[6pt] \frac{\partial}{\partial \mu} \log p &= \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma^2} \[6pt] \mathcal{I}(\mu) &= \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma^2}\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \end{aligned} $$

More variance in data ($\sigma^2$ large) → less Fisher information → harder to estimate $\mu$.

---

## Natural Gradient

Standard gradient descent ignores the geometry of parameter space.  
Natural gradient uses the FIM to account for it:

$$ \tilde{\nabla}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} L = \mathcal{I}(\boldsymbol{\theta})^{-1} \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} L $$

|Method|Update|
|---|---|
|Gradient descent|$\boldsymbol{\theta} \leftarrow \boldsymbol{\theta} - \eta \nabla L$|
|Natural gradient|$\boldsymbol{\theta} \leftarrow \boldsymbol{\theta} - \eta \mathcal{I}^{-1} \nabla L$|

Natural gradient = steepest descent in the space of distributions (not parameter space).  
More invariant to reparameterization — converges faster in theory.

**In practice:** FIM is too expensive to compute and invert for large models.  
Adam approximates the diagonal of the FIM cheaply.

---

## Fisher Information and KL Divergence

Fisher information is the local curvature of KL divergence:

$$ D_{KL}(p(x;\theta) | p(x;\theta+\delta)) \approx \frac{1}{2} \delta^\top \mathcal{I}(\theta) \delta $$

Small parameter change $\delta$ → KL divergence ≈ quadratic form in FIM.  
This is why FIM appears in trust region methods (TRPO, PPO).

---

## Bittensor Pruning Score

Fisher information is used to score neuron importance:

$$ \text{Score}(w_i) = \mathcal{I}(w_i) \cdot w_i^2 = \left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_i}\right)^2 \cdot w_i^2 $$

High score = parameter carries a lot of information about the loss → keep it.  
Low score = parameter is unimportant → prune it.
