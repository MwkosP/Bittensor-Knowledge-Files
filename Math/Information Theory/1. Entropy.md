

# Information Theory — Entropy

---

## Core Idea

Entropy measures **uncertainty** or **information content** in a distribution.  
High entropy = unpredictable. Low entropy = predictable.

---

## Shannon Entropy

For a discrete distribution $P$ over $K$ outcomes:

$$ H(X) = -\sum_{k=1}^K P(x_k) \log P(x_k) $$

|Convention|Base|Unit|
|---|---|---|
|$\log_2$|bits|Information theory|
|$\ln$ (natural log)|nats|ML / deep learning|

---

## Intuition

$$ \text{Information of event } x = -\log P(x) $$

- Rare event ($P \approx 0$) → high information — surprising
- Common event ($P \approx 1$) → low information — unsurprising

Entropy = **expected information** across all events.

---

## Example

Fair coin: $P(H) = P(T) = 0.5$

$$ H = -(0.5 \log 0.5 + 0.5 \log 0.5) = 1 \text{ bit} $$

Biased coin: $P(H) = 0.9$, $P(T) = 0.1$

$$ H = -(0.9 \log 0.9 + 0.1 \log 0.1) \approx 0.47 \text{ bits} $$

Certain outcome: $P(H) = 1.0$

$$ H = -(1.0 \cdot \log 1.0) = 0 $$

---

## Entropy of Common Distributions

|Distribution|Entropy|
|---|---|
|Uniform over $K$ classes|$\log K$ (maximum)|
|Peaked / certain|$\approx 0$ (minimum)|
|Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$|$\frac{1}{2}\ln(2\pi e \sigma^2)$|

Uniform distribution **maximizes** entropy — all outcomes equally likely = maximum uncertainty.

---

## Cross-Entropy

Measures how well distribution $Q$ approximates true distribution $P$:

$$ H(P, Q) = -\sum_k P(x_k) \log Q(x_k) $$

$$ H(P, Q) = H(P) + D_{KL}(P | Q) $$

Always: $H(P, Q) \geq H(P)$ — cross-entropy is minimized when $Q = P$.

---

## Cross-Entropy Loss in ML

The standard classification loss **is** cross-entropy between true labels and model predictions.

For one sample with true class $y$ and predicted distribution $\hat{y}$:

$$ L = -\sum_k y_k \log \hat{y}_k $$

For one-hot labels (only the true class $c$ has $y_c = 1$):

$$ L = -\log \hat{y}_c $$

For a full dataset of $N$ samples:

$$ L = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \log \hat{y}_{i,c_i} $$

**Minimizing cross-entropy = making the model's distribution match the true distribution.**

---

## Why Cross-Entropy and Not MSE for Classification?

|Loss|Problem|
|---|---|
|MSE on probabilities|Flat gradients near 0/1 — learning stalls|
|Cross-entropy|Large gradient when confident and wrong — fast learning|

Cross-entropy naturally penalizes confident wrong predictions very harshly:

$$ -\log(0.01) \approx 4.6 \quad \text{vs} \quad -\log(0.99) \approx 0.01 $$

---

## Binary Cross-Entropy

For binary classification ($y \in {0, 1}$, $\hat{y} \in (0,1)$):

$$ L = -[y \log \hat{y} + (1-y) \log(1-\hat{y})] $$

This is cross-entropy with $K=2$ classes.

---

## Conditional Entropy

Expected entropy of $Y$ given $X$:

$$ H(Y \mid X) = -\sum_{x,y} P(x,y) \log P(y \mid x) $$

Measures remaining uncertainty in $Y$ after observing $X$.

$$ H(Y \mid X) \leq H(Y) $$

Knowing $X$ can only reduce (or maintain) uncertainty about $Y$.

---

## Joint Entropy

$$ H(X, Y) = -\sum_{x,y} P(x,y) \log P(x,y) $$

$$ H(X, Y) = H(X) + H(Y \mid X) $$

---

## Entropy Relationships

$$ \begin{aligned} H(X,Y) &= H(X) + H(Y \mid X) \\ H(X,Y) &= H(Y) + H(X \mid Y) \\ H(Y \mid X) &\leq H(Y) \\ H(X,Y) &\leq H(X) + H(Y) \end{aligned} $$

---

## ML Connections

|ML Concept|Entropy Role|
|---|---|
|Cross-entropy loss|Standard classification loss|
|Softmax output entropy|High = uncertain model, low = confident|
|Decision tree splits|Split on feature that maximizes information gain = reduces entropy|
|Distillation|Soft targets have higher entropy → richer signal|
|VAE|Entropy term in ELBO|
|Regularization via entropy|Maximize prediction entropy to prevent overconfidence|

### Information Gain (Decision Trees)

$$ \text{IG}(Y, X) = H(Y) - H(Y \mid X) $$

Split on the feature that reduces entropy the most.
