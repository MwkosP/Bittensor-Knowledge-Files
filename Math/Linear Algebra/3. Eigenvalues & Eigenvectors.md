



# Linear Algebra — Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

---

## Core Idea

Most vectors change direction when multiplied by a matrix.  
**Eigenvectors don't** — they only get scaled.

$$ A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v} $$

|Symbol|Meaning|
|---|---|
|`A`|Square matrix `n × n`|
|`v`|Eigenvector — direction that doesn't change|
|`λ`|Eigenvalue — how much v gets scaled|

---

## Simple Example

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

$$ A\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = 3 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

So `v = [1, 0]ᵀ` is an eigenvector with eigenvalue `λ = 3`.

---

## How to Find Eigenvalues

Rearrange `Av = λv`:

$$ (A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} $$

For a non-trivial solution, the matrix `(A - λI)` must be singular:

$$ \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 $$

This is the **characteristic equation**. Solving it gives the eigenvalues.

### Example (2×2):

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} $$

$$ \det\left(\begin{bmatrix} 4-\lambda & 1 \ 2 & 3-\lambda \end{bmatrix}\right) = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = 0 $$

$$ \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0 \implies \lambda_1 = 5, \quad \lambda_2 = 2 $$

---

## How to Find Eigenvectors

For each eigenvalue λ, solve `(A - λI)v = 0`.

For `λ₁ = 5`:

$$ (A - 5I)\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \ 2 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \ v_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} \implies \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

---

## Key Properties

|Property|Rule|
|---|---|
|Trace|`tr(A) = Σλᵢ` — sum of eigenvalues|
|Determinant|`det(A) = Πλᵢ` — product of eigenvalues|
|Symmetric matrices|All eigenvalues are **real**|
|Positive definite|All eigenvalues `λ > 0`|
|Singular matrix|At least one `λ = 0`|

---

## Eigendecomposition

If A has n linearly independent eigenvectors, it can be decomposed as:

$$ A = Q \Lambda Q^{-1} $$

$$ Q = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & \\ \mathbf{v}_1 & \mathbf{v}_2 & \cdots \\ | & | & \end{bmatrix}, \quad \Lambda = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \ddots \end{bmatrix} $$

For **symmetric** matrices: `A = QΛQᵀ` (Q is orthogonal, so `Q⁻¹ = Qᵀ`).

---

## Geometric Intuition

$$ \begin{aligned} \lambda > 1 &\quad \text{stretches in that direction} \\ 0 < \lambda < 1 &\quad \text{compresses in that direction} \\ \lambda < 0 &\quad \text{flips and scales} \\ \lambda = 0 &\quad \text{collapses that direction (rank loss)} \end{aligned} $$

The **largest eigenvalue** = the direction of maximum variance in the data.
